## Why Your Digital Employee Isn't Producing Value?

Over the past six months, I've seen too many companies spend hundreds of thousands on AI systems that end up gathering dust.

I've also seen too many bosses excitedly say "I'm going to get digital employees," only to ask three months later: "How do I actually use this thing?"

Where's the problem?

**You bought digital employees like software, but you don't know how to "lead a team."**

Traditional software: you buy it, install it, train your staff, done.
Digital employees: "hiring" them is just the beginning—you need to train them, guide them, manage them, evaluate them.

90% of companies haven't figured out this logic.

So today, I'm sharing the pitfalls I've encountered and the methods I've summarized over the past two years as a **"Five-Layer Model of Digital Employee Management."**

This isn't theory—it's bought with real money and countless late nights.

## The Five-Layer Model of Digital Employee Management

### Layer 1: How to Build a Digital Employee

**Keywords: Selection, Setup, Integration**

Many bosses immediately ask: "Which AI is the best?"

That question itself is wrong. You should be asking: "What problem do I need to solve?"

I've seen a classic counterexample: a company spent 2 million yuan on a "full-featured AI platform," only to find that 80% of the features were useless, and the features they actually needed required secondary development.

**The first step in building a digital employee is defining its "job description."**

Just like hiring a real employee:
- What problem does this role need to solve?
- What skills are required?
- Which systems does it need to collaborate with?
- What are the output standards?

My content collection digital employee initially did just one thing: every day at 9 AM, collect the latest articles from 10 designated sources and organize them into a document in a fixed format.

That simple.

Later, it gradually added features like "preliminary screening," "keyword extraction," and "popularity analysis."

**Remember: First make it able to work, then make it work well.**

For technology selection, I have a principle: **Use open-source over closed-source; deploy locally over cloud.**

Not to save money, but for control.

Your digital employee needs to access your internal data, understand your business logic, and meet your security requirements. Closed-source solutions struggle with these.

My current tech stack:
- Core framework: OpenClaw (open-source content collection)
- Model layer: Locally deployed open-source models + cloud API hybrid
- Storage layer: Local SQLite + vector database
- Scheduling layer: Simple cron tasks + status monitoring

Not flashy, but stable, controllable, and iterable.

### Layer 2: How to Form a Digital Employee

**Keywords: Training,磨合 (磨合 = breaking-in/collaboration), Trust**

After a digital employee "starts work," the most easily ignored phase begins.

When you hire a real person, there's a probation period, right? Someone needs to mentor them, and they need time to learn the business, right?

Same with digital employees.

But I've seen too many companies deploy today, expect output tomorrow, and ask "why no results?" the day after.

**Forming a digital employee is essentially a process of "training + breaking-in."**

My content collection digital employee operated in a "human + automated" hybrid mode for the first month:
- It collects, I screen
- It organizes, I revise
- It labels, I review

Every time I modify its output, I'm "feeding it data." After three months, its draft acceptance rate increased from 30% to 85%.

Core actions in this phase:

1. **Build a feedback loop**: Every time it produces output, give feedback—what's good, what's not, and why
2. **Accumulate training data**: Your revisions, labels, and evaluations are all learning materials for it
3. **Allow mistakes**: The first 10, 20 times, it will definitely make mistakes. This is normal; don't否定 it because of this

I have a habit: I spend 1 hour every week "reviewing" with my digital employee.

Looking at what it did this week, what went well, what can be improved. This action is exactly the same as leading a real team.

**Trust is built through breaking-in, not through deployment.**

---

### Layer 3: How to Guide a Digital Employee

**Keywords: Direction, Goals, Boundaries**

The digital employee can work now, and the breaking-in phase went well. What's next?

**You need to give it direction.**

Real employees need to know "where the company is heading this year." Digital employees need the same.

My content collection digital employee initially just "collected articles." Later, I gave it new directions:
- Focus on AI cases in state-owned enterprise digital transformation
- Pay attention to policy trends and industry standards
- Mark content that can be used as course materials

This is "guiding"—telling it which direction to work toward, what's more important.

**The core of guiding is letting it understand your "strategic intent."**

Real employees rely on communication, meetings, and culture. What do digital employees rely on?

On the "prompts" you give them, the "evaluation criteria" you set, the "priorities" you assign.

I have a specific practice: every quarter, I update its "work manual" once.

This manual isn't a technical document; it's a "business understanding document":
- What are our priorities this quarter?
- Which content is more important?
- Who will use the outputs, and what do they care about?

Sounds "vague," but digital employees really can understand.

**Boundaries are equally important.**

You need to clearly tell it: what can be done, what cannot be done; what requires approval, what can be decided autonomously.

My digital employee has one iron rule: **Any content to be published externally must go through human review.**

This isn't distrust; it's risk control.

---

### Layer 4: How to Lead a Digital Employee

**Keywords: Collaboration, Empowerment, Growth**

At this layer, the digital employee is no longer a "tool"—it's a "team member."

**Leading a digital employee follows the same underlying logic as leading a real team.**

First, **let it collaborate with other members**.

My digital employee isn't isolated; it's deeply integrated into my workflow:
- Content it collects goes directly into my knowledge base
- Reports it organizes are automatically pushed to my to-do list
- Materials it marks, I periodically review and decide how to use

It's not an "add-on"; it's "embedded."

Second, **give it appropriate empowerment**.

I now allow my digital employee to autonomously decide:
- Which articles deserve to be marked as "high priority"
- Which content can skip human screening and go directly to the next stage
- Which data anomalies require proactive alerts to me

Empowerment's prerequisite is trust; trust's prerequisite is breaking-in.

Third, **let it continue growing**.

Digital employees aren't static. As the business changes, they must change too.

Every month, I ask myself three questions:
- What aspects of its current work can be optimized?
- Are there new tasks I can assign to it?
- Can its capability boundaries be expanded a bit more?

Last month, I added "preliminary analysis" capability to it—not just collecting, but also providing simple trend judgments.

It did it.

**The essence of leadership is making team members better. Digital employees are no different.**

---

### Layer 5: How to Evaluate a Digital Employee

**Keywords: Value, ROI, Iteration**

The final layer, and the most commonly asked: "Is this thing worth it?"

When evaluating digital employees, I never look at "how long it's been working" or "how much it's produced." I look at: **What value has it created?**

My evaluation framework has three dimensions:

**1. Efficiency Improvement**
- How much time has it saved you?
- What higher-value activities have you used that time for?

My content collection digital employee saves me about 10 hours per week. I use that time to develop new courses, write books, and meet clients.

**2. Quality Improvement**
- How does its output compare to what humans produce?
- Has it discovered insights that humans might easily miss?

Honestly, in terms of "comprehensiveness," it has already surpassed me. I can monitor at most 20 sources; it can monitor 100 simultaneously.

**3. Opportunity Creation**
- Because of it, have you accomplished things you couldn't before?
- Because of it, have you seized opportunities you would have missed?

This dimension is the hardest to quantify, but the most important.

I have a client who used a digital employee for competitor monitoring and discovered a competitor's new product movement two weeks early, adjusted their strategy, and made an extra 3 million yuan.

Do you think that digital employee was worth it?

**The purpose of evaluation isn't to "score"; it's to "iterate."**

After each evaluation, I ask: Can it be better? Where can it improve? Should we adjust its direction?

**Digital employees don't "graduate"; they only "continuously grow."**

## Case Study: A Digital Employee's Growth Path

Let me use a specific case to tie the five-layer model together.

**Background**: June 2024, I decided to build a "content collection digital employee"

**Month 1 (Build)**:
- Defined requirements: Collect industry articles daily, organize into documents
- Technology selection: OpenClaw + local models
- Deployment: Basic workflow operational

**Months 2-3 (Form)**:
- Human screening + automated collection hybrid mode
- Recorded every modification, formed training data
- Draft acceptance rate increased from 30% to 60%

**Months 4-6 (Guide)**:
- Adjusted direction: Focus on state-owned enterprise digital transformation cases
- Added priority marking functionality
- Established "work manual" to clarify business understanding

**Months 7-9 (Lead)**:
- Empowered it to autonomously judge high-priority content
- Deeply integrated with workflow
- Added "preliminary analysis" capability

**Months 10-12 (Evaluate)**:
- Saves 10 hours per week
- Draft acceptance rate at 85%
- Discovered 3 important industry trends, directly used for course development

**Now**: It's still growing. Next step, I plan to let it attempt "draft writing."

## Action: A Checklist You Can Use Tomorrow

Reading this, you might be thinking: "How do I get started?"

Don't worry, I've got a checklist for you. You don't need to do everything at once; just start with the first item:

**□ 1. Define your first digital employee's "job description"**
- What problem does it need to solve?
- What skills are required?
- What are the output standards?

**□ 2. Choose your tech stack**
- Prioritize open-source
- Prioritize local deployment
- Prioritize controllability

**□ 3. Establish a feedback mechanism**
- Give feedback on every output
- Record modifications and evaluations
- Review once a week

**□ 4. Clarify direction and boundaries**
- What are this quarter's priorities?
- What can be done, what cannot?
- What requires approval, what can be autonomous?

**□ 5. Design an evaluation framework**
- How to measure efficiency improvement?
- How to judge quality improvement?
- How to discover opportunity creation?

**Remember: Don't pursue perfection; just start.**

Your first digital employee might be crude. That's okay. If it saves you 1 hour, that's value.

Then, let it grow.

## Conclusion: The Essence of AI Leadership

Writing this, I want to share a view that might offend some people:

**Many companies say "AI is useless," but actually "their leadership is insufficient."**

You lead a real team: you know to recruit, train, guide, manage, and evaluate.
You lead a digital employee: how can you expect "automatic output after deployment"?

The essence of AI leadership isn't how many AI tools you can use.

**It's treating AI as "people," leading it the way you lead a team, letting it truly integrate into your organization, and creating real value.**

This five-layer model is what I gained from stumbling over the past two years.

Hope it helps you avoid some detours.
